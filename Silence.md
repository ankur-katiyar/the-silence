# Silence  
*May 26, 2003*

## Prologue

There are wounds that bleed for a while and then fade with time.  
And then there are wounds that do not bleed at all.

They live quietly inside a person—hidden beneath breath, beneath thought, beneath every passing moment. They do not scream. They do not ask for attention.

They simply remain.

People often believe that hatred begins with anger, that violence is born from cruelty. But sometimes, it begins with something far quieter.

Sometimes, it begins with silence.

The kind of silence that follows loss.  
The kind that grows in the empty spaces where love once lived.  
The kind that slowly fills a man until he can no longer hear his own conscience.

This is not a story about terror.

It is a story about how terror is born.

It is a story about a boy who once dreamed of serving his country…  
about a son who loved his mother more than anything in the world…  
and about how grief, when left alone in the dark, can slowly turn into something unrecognizable.

This is a story about silence.

---

## Silence  

The sky was overcast. Winds swept gently through my hair. I waited, hoping to feel the first drops of rain upon my forehead. Somehow, it always made me feel closer to **Him**.

Just a few yards away, children were playing. Watching them was a quiet delight. Their minds were free of worry—no battles to fight, no burdens to carry. And suddenly, I found myself envying them.

I remembered the days when I, too, would spend entire afternoons playing beneath the blazing sun. When I returned home, my mother would scold me for staying out so long. Yet the moment she saw tears forming in my eyes, she would pull me into her arms. It was pure love—untouched, untainted. She was always there to protect me.

But now, I felt terribly alone and exposed. I could no longer feel her presence.

She died during the riots in the valley. I was only a teenager then. I had gone away for trials to join the army. On my way back home, I saw the entire neighborhood engulfed in flames.

I quickened my pace.

Soon I was running—gasping for breath.

I never realized that what awaited me would haunt me for the rest of my life.

The image of her lying in a pool of blood beside the Quran burned itself into my memory. She had been praying when they ransacked the locality. Those who managed to flee survived only to recount the horror—the brutality, the insanity, the moment when humanity seemed to descend to its lowest depths. How people could abandon every boundary for the sake of religion.

Strangely, I never cried.

Even as she was buried, I stood there without expression. My mind had gone blank. The silence inside me drowned out the wails of my relatives. That silence pierced my mind and my heart. It killed my youth.

Iron had entered my soul.

What followed was my attempt to avenge my mother’s death. I never knew whether I was right or wrong. All I knew was that the silence never ended. It haunted me—every day, every minute, every second. I could not escape it.

I barely remember what happened after my mother died. Those memories are the kind a man would wish to forget. I had deliberately murdered the humanity within me. I hid it behind the brutal mask of terror.

Only later did I realize that it was not a shield.

It was a death chamber.

One that slowly suffocated the man inside me.

---

They came one night—one of the many nights I spent battling the silence that plagued my mind.

“I am **Imraan Murshid**, the leader of *Mulk-e-Sirat*. Ever heard of it?”

One of them spoke. He wore a black robe that concealed most of his body and face. Only his dark brown eyes were visible.

There was anguish in them.

For some reason, I recognized that pain. It was the same pain that had crippled me—left me spiritually crippled. Yet now I had someone offering me purpose.

Someone who wanted my help.

Help to avenge my mother’s death.

To see fear in the eyes of those who had taken away the only person in the world who had truly been there for me.

---

The next two years were the darkest of my life.

I had become blind.

I saw the world through the eyes of men who believed only one thing:

**“To kill is to win. And to win is to live.”**

I became the face of evil.

And the more I killed, the lonelier I became.

I could feel my mother drifting further away from me. I could see tears in her eyes—the same tears she had shed after scolding me as a child.

Yet the silence remained.

Perhaps I was not finished yet, I told myself. Perhaps I needed to kill more.

And so I continued.

After two years of bloodshed, I became a wanted man. A bounty of **one lakh rupees** was placed on my head. I was now the most feared terrorist in the valley.

People would glance at my photograph in newspapers and shudder.

“Oh my God… he is a born terrorist. Look at his eyes. I’m sure he sees nothing but death.”

They never realized that behind those eyes was once a boy who dreamed of serving his country—who loved his motherland with pride and devotion.

Eyes that had once known only love.

---

I had infiltrated **Baramulla** two days earlier. I was there for a mission—as Murshid called it.

But I knew the truth.

I was there to fight a battle that was never mine.

A battle with no enemies.

Only brothers.

And I was there to kill.

Rain began to fall.

The laughter of the children in the distance slowly faded. They had no idea that just a few yards away stood a monster—a creature of terror.

Behind my robe hung an automatic rifle.

Enough ammunition to wipe out an entire village.

I stood in the veranda of the local school. It was an old building—two rooms and a small garden in front. The children continued playing despite the rain.

**Shahid** stood beside me. He was my aide for the mission.

We had come to assassinate the local MLA—one who had ignored repeated warnings from terrorist outfits and dared to contest the by-elections.

“Must be quite a man,” Shahid said in his Pakistani accent.

Shahid had crossed into Kashmir two years earlier after receiving basic training in a militant camp. He was two years younger than me.

I had grown fond of him.

Often I urged him to return home.

“What would I do there, bhaijaan? There is no life, no respect. Why should I go back?”

No life there?

As if we had one here.

Every morning we woke up uncertain whether we would live to see another sunrise.

---

The school served as our hideout until the day of the attack.

Shahid had inspected the weapons an hour earlier. Everything had to be perfect. We would get only one opportunity.

From a small hill overlooking the road, I was to shoot the MLA as his car passed the next morning.

Thirty seconds.

That was all the time I would have.

Miss the shot—and his security would tighten beyond reach.

But I never thought about such things before a mission.

I had only one goal:

To silence the silence.

Yet it never left me.

---

The rain grew heavier. The distant village blurred beneath the gray sky. The children ran under the veranda for shelter. Their faces were flushed from exertion, their laughter priceless.

Suddenly Shahid shouted,

“Bhai! They are coming!”

His face had turned pale with dread.

A chill ran down my spine.

I was never afraid of death. I had always known the inevitable day would come. But I had not yet found peace.

And I did not want to die like this.

I ran into one of the rooms where ammunition was stored. Shahid followed, along with six children who had run inside.

Their faces held no fear—only curiosity, as though something exciting was unfolding.

The children of the valley usually grew up with terror in their backyard.

Yet these children did not understand what was about to happen.

“Not the children, Shahid. Leave them aside,” I said while loading my rifle.

“They will blow us up with grenades if they know we are alone. Even if we don’t want to, we must keep them as hostages.”

He was right.

If the army knew we were alone, they would destroy the school without hesitation.

“Alright. Keep one child. Let the rest go.”

Shahid looked at them.

None moved.

Now they understood.

Fear finally appeared in their eyes.

The same fear I had seen countless times in the eyes of those I had killed.

“You—come here. The rest of you, leave before they arrive,” Shahid ordered, forcing his voice to sound harsher than he felt.

But the children shook their heads.

“We will stay with **Abu**. We cannot leave him. Please let us stay.”

Their voices rose together in desperate unity.

“No. You must go. Your lives are in danger,” I insisted.

But when I looked into their eyes…

I saw love.

The same love I had once seen in my mother’s eyes.

---

The army had arrived.

Their alarm echoed across the valley.

Within minutes they surrounded the school.

“We know you are inside. If you want to live, come out!”

The amplified voice of an army officer thundered through a loudspeaker.

The youngest child began to cry.

Dusk slowly descended over Baramulla. The boy longed to return to his mother. His tears fell onto the floor scattered with bullets.

Shahid prepared for a gunfight.

But I found myself staring at the child.

Into his eyes.

They were beautiful.

Filled with tears.

And somehow—through the roar of helicopters and the chaos around me—I could still hear him crying.

The silence inside me…

was beginning to fade.

For the first time in years, I felt peace.

Now I understood why I had never found it.

---

“Bhaijaan!”

Shahid screamed.

A bullet had struck him below the kidney.

He collapsed almost instantly.

Blood spread across the floor.

I rushed to him and lifted him into my lap. His eyes were filled with agony.

He wanted to live.

“Bhai… I don’t want to die,” he whispered.

“Yes, you will live, Shahid. You will return to Pakistan. You will live with your family.”

Tears filled my eyes.

It had been ages since I last cried.

It felt as though the man who had died with my mother had risen again from the ashes.

My tears fell upon Shahid’s lifeless face.

But he did not move.

Perhaps he had found the peace that had always eluded him here.

---

I looked toward the children.

They stood silently in the corner, exhausted from crying.

“Go. Run. Leave now!”

They fled.

I knew the inevitable would soon come.

I lay beside Shahid’s body, weary from running all my life.

I had tried so desperately to silence the deafening quiet within me. Yet all along, I had been running away from it.

It was almost ironic.

As I stared at the roof, I saw my mother’s eyes.

I felt her gentle hand on my forehead.

I heard the lullabies she used to sing to help me sleep.

The silence was gone.

Her voice filled everything.

For the first time in years,

I felt peace.

And then…

I fell asleep.
